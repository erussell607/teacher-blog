MH Step 3 – Objective(s)

Creating unique descriptions of characters and objects using descriptive language.

MH Step 4 – Teacher Input/ Modeling (I do)

The teacher will put the piece of gum in his mouth and will then begin describing the gum in his mouth with vivid words.

MH Step 5 – Checking for Understanding

The teacher will ask which describing words stood out to them

MH Step 6 – Guided Practice (We do)

Give the whole class a sour patch kid, and then call on the students to describe what they taste while providing feedback to their responses.

Next, give each student an altoid. After the students finish their altoid, have each student write down the experience that they had eating the altoid and share with a partner.

MH Step 7 – Independent Practice (You do)

The teacher will give several students Oreo cookies, and the ask for volunteers to eat a prune instead. The students will eat their snack, and then they will use vivid words to describe what they have eaten to the other students.

MH Closure

Have the students reflect on the activity in their journals by writing at least two sentences using at least six vivid vocabulary words, and then allow students to share aloud.

PART V: REFLECTION

Reflection

Reflect on your lesson planning experience. What challenged you? What did you learn?

Creating this lesson in a way to ensure student success as well as easily structuring it for the teacher was a good experience. One major challenge was not knowing the actual group of students, so it was hard to shape the lesson as well as the accommodations and modifications around “potential” students. Although I have written lesson plans previously, I learned how detailed some things need to be because although you know what you want the outcome to be, if anyone else read it they may not know what your goal is. Anyone should be able to pick up the lesson plan and implement it. Another thing I learned is how important it is to know your students so that you know where to start the lesson. You can activate prior knowledge, but you still need to know where to go from there, as well as making the lesson relevant to your group of students. Knowing their interests, strengths, and weaknesses can help form a well-rounded lesson that keeps the students interested and engaged.
